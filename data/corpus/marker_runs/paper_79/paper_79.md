#### **Abstract**

In this study, the static and dynamic properties of shape memory polyurethane (SMPU) fiber are reported and compared to those of spider dragline silk. Although the polymeric fiber has a lower strength compared to spider dragline silks (0.2–0.3 GPa versus 1.1 GPa), it possesses much higher toughness (276–289 MJ m<sup>-3</sup> versus 160 MJ m<sup>-3</sup>), due to its excellent extensibility. The dynamic mechanical tests reveal that SMPU fiber has a high damping capacity (tan  $\delta$ =0.10–0.35) which is comparable to or even higher than that of spider silks (tan  $\delta$ =0.15). In addition, we found that, different programming methods change the shape memory and damping properties of the fiber in different ways and cold-drawing programming is more advocated in structural applications. These results suggest that the SMPU fiber has similar vibration damping and mechanical properties as spider silk, and may find applications in lightweight engineering structures.

#### 1. Introduction

Advanced lightweight composite structures have found widespread applications in various industries. As compared to heavy-weight structures, however, light-weight structures can be vibrated easily (while other conditions being the same, e.g., stiffness and geometry). Due to the need for structural stability, position control, durability, performance, and noise reduction, vibrations are undesirable for structures [1]. Therefore, vibration damping in lightweight structures is highly desired. Mechanical vibrations can be reduced in many ways, for instance, by using external dampers. Such kind of damping strategies can be seen everywhere in our daily life: shock absorbers are used in vehicles to smooth out impulse and dissipate kinetic energy [2], and a shock bridge damper is a tuned mass damper used to suppress wind-induced vibrations on taut cables, including overhead power lines [3]. Usually, external dampers exhibit higher damping efficiency for structures with higher inertial mass. For lightweight structures, however, damping by external damper only is very challenging. Therefore, seeking materials with combined features of high strength, light weight, low cost and especially high damping capacity has become a popular topic for the last decades.

Human often seeks inspiration from nature. Spider silk is one example of the superior natural materials. Within different types of spider silks, the spider dragline silk, which is produced in the major ampullate gland of a spider and is used for the frame and radii of an orb web, is the most investigated one [4]. As the frame of the orb web, dragline silk must be extremely strong and tough to absorb the tremendous kinetic energy of insect prey. Therefore, most spider silks have an unusual combination of strength and extensibility, which leads to toughness values seldom attained in synthetic materials. At the same time, webs must also minimize the return of the kinetic energy of the prey to prevent insects from bouncing out of oscillating webs. Furthermore, unlike bungee jumper who vibrates drastically, a spider that drops with its

<span id="page-2-0"></span>silk thread hardly ever oscillates [4–6]. All these observations indicate that energy dissipation in the spider silk is efficient, namely, it has a high damping capacity. In addition to the aforementioned outstanding mechanical and dynamic properties, spider silk also possesses super-contraction upon exposure to humidity. This contraction stress may maintain tension in webs under the weight of rain or dew and could be used in industry for robotics, sensor technology, and other applications [4, 7–9].

The extraordinary properties of spider dragline are determined by its unique molecular structure. It is now well accepted that spider dragline is a semicrystalline material made of amorphous flexible chains reinforced by strong and stiff crystals. It is the perfect balance between the high stiffness of crystalline and the high ductility of the amorphous phase that gives the spider silk excellent mechanical properties [6, 10].

Although the molecular structure of the spider silk has been well studied in the literature, there are no commercially available spider silks. Harvesting silks from spiders directly is complex, as spiders cannot be housed in high densities due to their aggressive nature and overall small yield of silk. Silks have been cloned and expressed in a variety of heterologous hosts, from bacteria and fungi to plants and mammals, as well as via transgenic silk-worms, and yet the material properties of these cloned silks are still inferior to the native materials [11]. Therefore, synthesis of polymeric fibers with similar mechanical properties, such as stiffness and damping capabilities are highly desired.

Shape memory polymer is a class of smart materials that can be deformed and fixed in a temporary shape under a certain condition and recovers the original shape when exposed to a suitable stimulus. In recent decades, shape memory polymer has emerged as one of the key functional materials in both academia and industry due to its low cost, light weight and high shape recoverability [12–17]. Shape memory effect can be realized in various polymer systems that are significantly different in molecular structure and morphology [18], and among which, shape memory polyurethane (SMPU) draws the most attention due to easy control of its critical recovery temperature and mechanical properties [19, 20]. It has been demonstrated that constrained shape recovery (shrinkage) of cold-drawing programmed SMPU fiber, which is embedded in a conventional thermosetting polymer matrix, can repeatedly close wide-opened (millimeter scale) cracks in the matrix due to its shape memory functionality [21–24]. From a microscopic point of view, polyurethanes are usually composed of a flexible polyether or polyester soft segment and a stiff diisocyanate-based hard segment, which are thermodynamically immiscible with each other [25, 26]. Considering the superficial similarity in microstructures, we envision that this SMPU fiber would also have a damping capacity comparable to spider silk and at the same time keep a relatively high stiffness and strength. In a previous study, we have performed several qualitative experiments, indicating the remarkable damping properties and impact tolerance of such kind of polymeric fibers as compared to other synthetic fibers, such as carbon fibers [21]. The objective of this study is to investigate the effect of programming on the mechanical and damping properties of SMPU fibers. We will first analyze the morphology of the SMPU fiber based on dynamic scanning calorimeter (DSC) results. Then, the thermomechanical properties of the SMPU fiber with both cold and hot tension programming will be investigated. As-spun SMPU fiber, which is the manufactured fiber without further thermal and mechanical treatments, will be used as control sample. We will then focus on the damping capacity which depends on temperature, frequency and programming procedures. Finally, a comparison with spider dragline silk in various aspects will be given.

# 2. Experimental

#### 2.1. Materials

In this study, the polyurethane was synthesized using poly (butylene adipate) (PBA) as soft segments, 4'4-diphenylmethane diisocyanate (MDI) and 1,4-butanediol (BDO) as hard segment. The average formula weight ratio of (MDI+BDO): PBA = 1021:650. The glass transition temperature of pure amorphous PBA was reported in the ATHAS database 'Advanced Thermal Analysis System Data Bank (ATHAS-DB)' [27], and was as low as -55 °C. For the hard segments, the glass transition temperature of pure amorphous sample was detected around 110 °C [28] and the crystalline melting transition was above 200 °C [29].

Polyurethane was prepared by the pre-polymerization technique and polyurethane fiber was prepared from the polyurethane using a melt spinning method by the OneShot spinning machine at a spinning speed of 40 m min<sup>-1</sup>. The spinning temperature is 190 °C. For more information about the copolymer and the spinning technique, please refer to [18, 30, 31].

# 2.2. Programming of SMPU fiber

When studying the shape memory polymers, programming, that is, thermomechanical treatments, is the key process to give the material 'memory'. In this study, an MTS (Alliance RT/5, MTS, USA) equipped with a temperature regulated oven was used to program the samples. In the tests, a 250 N load cell and a pair of clamps specified for polymer fibers (Manual Bollard Grips, MTS) were used (see the supplementary material figure S1 for the pictures of the equipment system). Two different programming temperatures were used and the fibers were divided into three groups based on their programming history. The non-programmed fiber, denoted as sample #1, was kept at room temperature and was not stretched. This sample was used as the control sample. Sample #2 was programmed by stretching the fiber bundle (100 filaments) to 100% at temperature of 100 °C, holding it at 100 °C for 1 h, cooling down to the room temperature while maintaining the strain constant, and finally removing the load. 100 °C was chosen because it is above the mixed phase transition temperature, while below the hard phase transition

#### 2.3. DSC analysis

In order to investigate the transition behavior and the morphologies of the samples, a PerkinElmer DSC4000 power compensated DSC was used to measure the heat flow of the material as a function of temperature. Based on the well-known DSC testing procedure [32, 33], the heat flow can be used to determine the transition temperatures. In each test, 4–6 mg chopped fibers (about 2 mm long) was quenched to –60 °C, and then heated up to 250 °C, which is above the reported pure hard segment melting temperature. After melting at high temperature for 1 min, this cooling-heating cycle was repeated one more time. The cooling rate was set to be 20 °C min<sup>-1</sup> and the heating rate was 10 °C min<sup>-1</sup>. Figure 1 summarizes DSC protocol. All the data have been normalized by the weight of the tested samples and each type of sample was tested at least three times.

#### 2.4. SEM observation

The cross-sectional diameters of all the three samples were obtained using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, FEI Quanta 200). The fibers were coated with platinum before observation. Three pictures were taken and the average values were used for determining the diameter of each sample.

#### 2.5. Mechanical properties

In order to measure the mechanical properties of each sample, uniaxial tensile tests were performed for all three samples at room temperature on the mechanical testing system described in section 2.2. The gauge length was set to be 10 mm and the

cross head speed was 200 mm min<sup>-1</sup>. Load verses engineering strain curves were recorded. For each sample, at least four specimens were tested.

### 2.6. Shape memory properties

Fully constrained stress recovery tests (recover while maintaining strain zero) were performed for the programmed samples (samples #2 and #3) on the MTS system described in section 2.2, and recovery stresses were used to quantify the shape memory property of the samples. One hundred filaments as a bundle were gripped and fixed by the fixture. Each sample was heated up from room temperature to 150 °C with a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>-1</sup>, and then it was kept at the high temperature for 10 h until the recovery force was stabilized. This recovery temperature was chosen so that both soft and hard phase can take part in the recovery process. For both samples #2 and #3, the tests were repeated three times.

#### 2.7. Damping properties

In order to characterize the damping properties of the samples, isothermal strain controlled frequency sweep tests were performed for all three samples on a dynamic mechanical analysis (DMA) 2980 tester from TA instruments (see the supplementary material figure S2 for the pictures of the DMA system). Before the test, the fiber bundle, contained one hundred filaments was straightened but with zero external tensile force. Gauge length was set to be 15 mm and the applied dynamic strain was 1%, which is within the linear viscoelastic region of the samples. The isothermal test temperatures were set to be 25 °C, 50 °C, 75 °C and 100 °C, respectively and the frequency range was 1 Hz–25 Hz with an increment of 1 Hz. For each sample, the tests were repeated at least three times.

# 3. Results and discussions

#### 3.1. DSC Analysis and programming effects

3.1.1. General description of morphology based on DSC results. The morphologies of polyurethane copolymers have been studied for decades and many morphological models have been proposed based on these results [29, 34-39]. Generally speaking, the polymer is composed of pure hard phase, pure soft phase, and a mixed phase, in which hard and soft segments incorporated with each other. Figure 2(a) is a schematic representation of the morphological model as a visual aid for the reader. Depending on the hard and soft segment contents, the molecular weight and distribution, and the thermomechanical history, the general three-phase model may take different configurations. For example, the pure soft phase may disappear for some SMPUs, the pure hard phase may become the continuous phase, the three-phase may become a random mixture, etc. It is the versatile microstructures that make SMPUs have such diverse physical/mechanical properties.

<span id="page-4-0"></span>Figure 2. (a) Schematic representation of the morphology of PU copolymer. (b) Second cycle DSC curve.

To get a basic idea on the morphology of our polymer, the second cycle DSC test results are utilized. In the second cycle of DSC tests, the mechanical and thermal history during fabrication and programming have been erased, hence, the same curves were obtained in the second cycle DSC scan for all samples. A typical second cycle DSC curve is presented in figure 2(b). Two sets of transitions can be detected, corresponding to the mixed phase, represented by transitions between 0 °C and 100 °C, and the hard phase, consisting of hard segments with long range order, represented by transitions from 120 °C to 200 °C [29]. More specifically, from low temperature to high temperature, the transitions take place in the following order: (1) glass transition of the mixed phase, which is determined by soft and short hard segments together [28], is characterized by the step around  $T_{\rm gMP}$  $24.61 \pm 0.78$  °C; (2) phase separation (or phase de-mixing), during which short hard segments segregate from mixed phase and aggregate into micro-hard phase [29, 34, 38]. This transition is characterized by the exothermic peak at  $T_{\rm DMX}$  (70.34 ± 1.52 °C); (3) Disorder or melting of the ordered hard segments in the pure hard phase, starts from around 120 °C and peaks around  $T_{\rm M}$  (143.97 ± 1.59 °C) [29, 39, 40]; (4) phase mixing, driven by the thermodynamic force (entropy), during which hard segments dissolve into mixed phase again and is characterized by the endothermic peak at  $T_{\rm MX}$  (166.97 ± 1.29 °C) [6, 39, 40]. Since there is no significant sign of the pure soft phase transition, the pure soft phase in the general three-phase model in figure 2(a) disappears for our SMPU fiber.

3.2.1. Mechanical properties. The SEM images of the three samples are shown in figure 5. According to the images and the scales, the cross-sectional diameters of sample #1 is 60 μm and samples #2 and #3, after programming, are 50 μm and 55 μm, respectively.

Figure 6 shows the tensile stress–strain response of the three samples at room temperature. It is clear that the programming procedure increases the stiffness (slope of the stress–strain curve) and the strength (peak stress) of the materials. Further comparison of programmed samples (samples #2 and #3) shows that programming at higher temperature (sample #2) results in higher stiffness and strength than programming at room temperature (sample #3). However, the increase in strength and stiffness always comes as a result of reduction in extensibility.

It is known that molecular orientation can enhance stiffness of materials in the stretched direction. Also, because the molecular chains have already been highly extended during programming, it is easier to reach their extension limit by further stretching. Therefore, the programmed samples have higher stiffness and strength, but lower extensibility as compared to the non-programmed control sample (sample #1). As has been discussed in the previous section, the overall elongation of sample #2 is attributed mostly to the soft segments, while in sample #3, both soft and hard segments have been stretched during the programming procedure. 3.2.2. Stress recovery properties. Typical fully constrained stress recovery tests results are shown in figure [7](#page-7-0)(a). Based on our observation, during the fully constrained stress recovery test, the overall stress recorded is determined by three effects: thermal expansion, releasing of the 'stored stress' and stress relaxation. At the beginning of the test, since the temperature is not high enough to trigger the massive releasing of the 'stored stress', the dominating effect is thermal expansion. As temperature increases, two competitive mechanisms begin to dominate the total stress: On one hand, driven by the entropic force, the molecular chains tend to recoil and return to the original configuration, i.e., shape recovery. Due to the fully constrained boundary condition, the shape recovery is not allowed, leading to buildup of tensile stress (imagine a material with negative thermal expansion coefficient), which is considered to be the releasing of the stored stress. This tensile stress increases as the recovery process proceeds. On the other hand, once the fibers are in tension, molecules tend to align along the loading direction, leading to stress relaxation. Because the constrained stress recovery test has

the same test configuration as conventional stress relaxation test, it helps understand why stress relaxation is one of the major components in stress recovery test. The two mechanisms competed with each other until the microstructure reaches an equilibrium state and by that time, the recovery stress is stabilized.

The peak and final stresses for both the programmed samples are reported in figure 7(b). The results revealed that sample #2 has a higher peak recovery stress, while sample #3 has a higher stabilized recovery stress. The differences in the recovery behaviors of both samples can be explained by the difference in their microstructures we hypothesized before. We believed that sample #2 has higher amount of oriented soft segments and the soft segments have lower transition temperature, and thus the recovery process of sample #2 was triggered first. The slightly lower recovery temperature, together with the slightly higher stiffness (figure 6) of sample #2, makes it have a higher peak stress than sample #3. process leadings to higher stabilized stress. In the bio-inspired self-healing applications, a sustained force is needed to close the macroscopic crack [22, 24]. In other words, the stabilized recovery stress plays a more important role in self-healing process. Therefore, sample #3 is more desired in self-healing application and approaches that can provide higher stabilized recovery stress should be a topic for future studies.

#### 3.3. Damping properties

The ability for polymers to damp vibration persists in their viscoelastic nature [1, 47]. This damping capability is maximized within the glass transition region. Within this region, when the material is forced to vibrate, some molecules exhibit viscous flow while others remain stiff. Internal molecular friction thus increases, which results in heat buildup and dissipation of the input energy [48].

Several techniques were proposed for testing damping properties and can be roughly divided into three main categories [49, 50]: (1) non-resonance techniques, such as DMA [51, 52], dynamic mechanical thermal analysis [53], and hysteresis cycle test [5, 54, 55]; (2) decay of free vibration, where the history of motion is recorded and the loss factor is used to quantify the damping capability [56]. This approach requires the vibration amplitude to be measured, which represents an important limitation when applied to thin fibers. (3) Resonance techniques, which consist of exciting a mechanical system at its natural frequencies and analyzing the transfer function between excitation and motion to extract the damping parameters [57]. Different techniques use different quantities to characterize damping property. Commonly used quantities are loss tangent (tan  $\delta$ ), loss factor ( $\eta$ ), specific damping capacity  $(\Psi)$ , logarithmic decrement  $(\Delta)$ , and inverse quality factor (Q-1) [58].

In our study, the temperature and frequency dependency of the dynamic properties of SMPU fiber was obtained using DMA. Theoretically, to obtain the best damping property, it is desired to maximize the loss modulus E'. However, there is a limit. Maximizing E' results in lower elastic response and, therefore, poor physical and mechanical properties may result. Furthermore, both the storage modulus E' and loss modulus E' are sensitive to geometry [59]. Therefore,  $\tan \delta$  was used here as a quantitative representation of material ability to dissipate energy: higher value of  $\tan \delta$  means better damping properties.

3.3.1. General description of the damping property. In figure 8, each plot represents the isothermal dynamic frequency sweep test of a certain sample. Tan  $\delta$  reaches its maximum at  $T=50\,^{\circ}\text{C}$  and minimum at  $T=100\,^{\circ}\text{C}$  for all three samples. This is because 50 °C is the test temperature that is closest to their corresponding glass transition temperatures (around 40 °C, based on the DMA  $T_{\rm g}$  tests by our group members [21, 23, 60]), whereas  $T=100\,^{\circ}\text{C}$  is farthest away from the corresponding  $T_{\rm g}$ . The results also indicate that the loss tangent of the material has an order of 0.1, and can be as high as 0. 35.

3.3.2. Dependence of damping property on frequency. In figure 9, the tan  $\delta$  versus frequency curves were regrouped for the convenience of investigation and comparison. Each plot represents the results of all three samples at a certain temperature. The plots indicate that the loss tangent is controlled by temperature, frequency and programming process simultaneously. However, the dependence of the loss tangent on frequency has similar trends for all three samples at a certain temperature: at low temperature  $(T=25\,^{\circ}\text{C})$ ,  $\tan \delta$  decreases monotonically with respect to frequencies; at intermediate temperature  $(T=50\,^{\circ}\text{C}, 75\,^{\circ}\text{C})$ ,  $\tan \delta$  first increases then flattens, and finally decreases; at high temperature  $(T=100\,^{\circ}\text{C})$ , the curve is more flattened at lower frequencies and is close to zero at higher frequencies.

This phenomenon can be explained by time-temperature equivalent principle, that is, higher frequency is equivalent to lower temperature and vice versa. According to the DMA  $T_{\rm g}$ tests by our group member [21, 23, 60], at T = 25 °C, the fiber is in glassy state or at the beginning of the glass transition zone. Increasing frequency is equivalent to decreasing temperature, which will result in lower loss tangent. Similarly, at the intermediate temperature, 50 °C and 75 °C, the material is within the glass transition zone or at the end of the zone. Applying higher frequency load is equivalent to bringing the material back from the rubber elastic state to the glass transition zone, passing the glass transition region and finally entering the glassy state. Thus an upward trend of the loss tangent curve appeared at the beginning followed by a downward trend of loss tangent. At even higher temperature T=100 °C, the amorphous phase of the polymer is in pure rubber elastic state. Loss tangent stays close to zero within this region. To give a concrete description on how to relate the frequency dependence and temperature dependence of loss tangent, a sketch is illustrated in supplemental figure S5. Start from the tan  $\delta$  versus temperature sketch, following the four steps in the notation and corresponding steps in the figure, a schematic  $\tan \delta$  versus frequency curve can be obtained.

Although all the samples obey similar patterns, different programming methods differentiate the loss tangent curves of the three samples from each other. At lower temperature (25 °C), sample #1 has the lowest value of tan  $\delta$ , suggesting programming increases the damping capability of SMPU fibers, mostly due to higher orientation of the programmed samples. Meanwhile, since sample #2 has the lowest glass transition temperature, as suggested by our DSC results, at 25 °C, sample #2 is the closest to its glass transition region. Therefore, sample #2 has the highest value of  $\tan \delta$ . At intermediate temperature (50 °C and 75 °C), cold-drawn SMPU fiber (sample #3) exhibits the highest damping capability, followed by as-spun SMPU fiber (sample #1), and the least by the hot-drawn SMPU fiber (sample #2). This is most likely because sample #2 has been through the phase separation, while samples #1 and #3 have not. To be more specific, recovery mechanism has been triggered within this temperature region during the damping test. The recovery stress generated in sample #2 is mostly used to stretch the soft segments because de-mixing of the mixed phase in

On the other hand, in sample #3, since the hard and soft segments are still entangled with each other in the mixed phase, part of the recovery stress is consumed by overcoming the friction among these chains and pulling the short hard segments out of the mixed phase. In other words, in addition to the intrinsic damping property of the fiber itself, the mixed phase also acts as an extra damping source, which leads to higher  $\tan \delta$  for sample #3. For sample #1, although it also has the soft segment and short hard segment mixture (interphase), the recovery stress is very small [16]. Therefore, the extra stress to pull the short hard phase out of the mixture is small as compared to sample #3. As a result, the damping capacity of sample #1 is in between sample #2 and sample #3. At higher temperature (100 °C), although all the samples have entered the rubbery state, the same tendency as in 50 °C and 75 °C remains for the three samples. Again, this is due to the additional energy dissipation when pulling the remaining short hard segments out of the mixed phase.

According to the above analysis, the influence of the programming procedures on damping properties were embedded in two aspects: first, decrease (sample #2) or increase (sample #3) the overall value of loss tangent; second, shift the glass transition temperature to a lower (sample #2) or higher (sample #3) value. In supplementary figure S6, these two changes have been visualized in the  $\tan \delta$  versus temperature curves, and the differences were explained schematically.

3.4.4. Microstructure. The comparisons above indicate that our SMPU fiber has a lot in common with the spider silk.

These similarities between spider silk and SMPU fiber are determined by their resemblances in microstructure.

As stated in section 1, spider dragline silk is a semicrystalline material made of amorphous flexible chains reinforced by strong and stiff crystals. The amorphous chains are also linked together through hydrogen bonds (figure 10(a), redraw from figure 1(a), [6]). The crystals, made of hydrophobic polyalanine sequences arranged into hydrogenbonded beta-pleats, produce thin regions of locally high modulus, leading to the enormous stiffness of the overall material. Due to its mechanical stability, the crystals also act as net-points to keep the permanent shape during the super contraction procedure. The highly entangled amorphous phase, made of kinetically free oligopeptide chains rich in glycine, provides the fiber with outstanding extensibility. Also, the friction and resistance among the entangled molecular chains can dissipate the input energy efficiently and thus give the material an extraordinary damping property. It is believed that water can prevent the formation of the hydrogen bonds between the molecular chains. Hence, the Hbond can be interpreted as a switch that is responsible for the molecular mobility during the super contraction procedure [6, 21].

Similar microstructure of SMPU fiber is illustrated schematically in figure 10(b) [21]. The hard phase of the SMPU fiber ensures the high local stiffness and acts as the net-points to retain the stable permanent shape; after programming, the mixed phase mainly contains soft segments and short hard segments, gives its outstanding extensibility. The restriction among entangled soft and hard segments and

the highly oriented chains act as the source of damping. Heating up can increase the molecular mobility of the oriented molecular chains and makes them recoil and finally brings the fiber back to its original shape. Therefore, the fiber has the shape memory effect.

#### 4. Conclusion

Based on the above tests, measurement and characterization, the following conclusions can be drawn:

- (1) Similar to spider silk, our SMPU fiber possesses high damping ability, and at the same time has a fairly high strength and toughness. In addition, the polymer fiber has shape memory effect, and after tension programming, has super-contraction upon heating, similar to moisture triggered spider silk. These similarities between spider silk and SMPU fiber are determined by their resemblances in microstructure.
- (2) The shape memory property is characterized by the fully constrained recovery stress. Different programming procedures result in different recovery behaviors. The cold-drawn fiber (sample #3) can generate higher stabilized stress, with is desirable in self-healing applications.
- (3) Programming the polymer fiber can alter its mechanical and thermal properties. After programming, stiffness and strength can be increased while extensibility is decreased. Programming at high temperature (sample #2) lowers

<span id="page-12-0"></span>glass transition temperature and the overall damping ability. On the contrary, programming at low temperature (sample #3) increases the glass transition temperature and damping ability. These differences attribute to the energy dissipated by the recovery stress triggered during damping tests.

Considering the increased damping capability, which is useful in controlling vibration, and the increased stabilized recovery stress, which helps provide the sustained crack-closure force in self-healing applications, together with its time and energy efficiency process, cold-drawing programming of the SMPU fibers is advocated in structural applications.
